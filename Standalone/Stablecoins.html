How GlobalGoods Inc. Uses Stablecoins to Move Dollars Faster (and Why it Matters)

A short, practical story: a large multinational dealing in countries with unstable local currencies uses stablecoins to protect revenue and speed up payments. It’s not a magic trick—it's a set of tradeoffs. Here’s how it works, in plain language.

The setting: Republica del Peso

GlobalGoods Inc. sells toothpaste, engine oil, and farm equipment in a country we’ll call Republica del Peso. The local currency, the Peso, loses value quickly. Banks are slow.

“By the time local pesos travel through the banking system and convert to dollars, a large chunk of value is often gone. We needed a faster, predictable way to get paid in dollars.”
— GlobalGoods Treasury

How the payment flow actually works

Let’s compare the old system with the new, to see where stablecoins fit in.

Before: the traditional payment route

  • Distributors in Republica del Peso sell products and collect payment in local pesos.
  • Those pesos are deposited in a local commercial bank.
  • The bank converts them to U.S. dollars through the central bank.
  • After several days—and some fees—GlobalGoods receives fewer dollars than expected due to exchange rate losses and conversion delays.

Result: the company gets paid slowly, and loses value as the peso depreciates.

After: the stablecoin-enabled payment route

  • Distributors still collect pesos locally, but instead of wiring them through the slow banking chain, they sell pesos to a licensed crypto broker for USD stablecoins.
  • The broker transfers those stablecoins (for example, USDC or USDT) directly to GlobalGoods’ digital wallet.
  • Settlement happens in minutes, not days, and the company holds value in digital dollars fully backed by U.S. Treasuries and cash reserves.
  • GlobalGoods can then use those stablecoins to pay suppliers, convert to bank dollars when convenient, or move funds instantly between regions.

Result: the company gets paid faster, with value preserved in dollar terms rather than bleeding away in currency devaluation.

Why this works

Stablecoins act as a bridge: they provide access to the strength of the U.S. dollar without needing the local banking system to function well. They’re still digital dollars—just moving across a faster, programmable network.

How the use of stablecoins benefits the U.S. government

The most popular stablecoins, like USDC and USDT, are backed mainly by U.S. Treasury bills. That means for every stablecoin issued, the issuer usually holds an equivalent dollar in short-term U.S. debt.

This has an interesting side effect: as global demand for stablecoins grows, so does demand for U.S. Treasuries. It creates a new, steady market for American government debt—essentially a digital-age buyer base for U.S. bonds.

The U.S. government benefits because a guaranteed market for Treasury bills helps keep borrowing costs low. The more buyers there are (including stablecoin issuers), the less interest the government needs to offer to attract funds.

In plain terms: when the world holds digital dollars backed by Treasuries, it helps finance U.S. government debt at scale.

Is that monetizing the U.S. debt? Not directly—the Treasury isn’t printing money for this purpose. But it’s fair to say that stablecoins help extend the reach of the dollar and make U.S. debt more useful as a global financial product. In that sense, it’s a new form of indirect monetization—where market demand for digital dollars strengthens demand for U.S. debt itself.

The tradeoffs

  • Stablecoin issuers hold your dollars and earn the interest from Treasuries—you don’t.
  • If confidence in an issuer collapses, there could be a “run” as users rush to redeem coins for cash.
  • Regulators are still catching up—oversight and transparency vary by jurisdiction.

Yet, for corporations operating in unstable economies, the benefits are tangible: faster payments, less value lost to inflation, and simpler access to dollars without relying on fragile local banks.
