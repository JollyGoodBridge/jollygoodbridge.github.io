The Deal (Starting Position)

Full deal (North dealer): North is declarer in 3NT. Click to expand.

Hands (S H D C)

North:  K72 . QJ83 . 9432 . Q4
East:   JT8 . KT9 . 876  . T95
South:  AQ9 . A765 . AQT  . AK7
West:   6543 . 42  . KJ5  . J863

Quick Counting — How Many Quick Tricks Do We Have?

Before you shove the defenders around, count your immediate winners:

  • South (combined viewpoint): Spades — A, K (2 quick tricks); Hearts — A (maybe more in combination); Clubs — A, K (2 quick tricks); Diamonds — A (1 quick trick). That’s about 6 clear tricks and a few more depending on cross-ruffing or finesses.
  • We also note some “wasted points”: South's AK of clubs are powerful, but if declarer can force an opponent to lead clubs later, they might not be as useful as they look now. North's small club holding (Qx) becomes a nice entry if the opponents are later forced to lead clubs.

The Plan

The aim in this compact example is to clear the side suits so the defenders are left only with diamonds — yielding the “end play”. When only diamonds remain, the defender who is thrown in will be forced to give a trick (or lead through declarer's tenace).

Play (Illustrative Line)

Note: This is one illustrative line to reach the three-card ending described. Many equivalent permutations exist.

  1. Assume normal opening lead (for example, West leads a small spade). Declarer wins in South with the Ace of spades.
  2. Take the top spade honors (A then K) to remove spade losers.
  3. Play hearts from the dummy (North) towards South’s Ace to eliminate heart losers — cash QJ from North and then South's Ace. This clears hearts.
  4. Cash clubs: cash South's A and K of clubs. If opponents follow, continue discarding any remaining side losers.
  5. During this process declarer manages entries so that at the moment only diamonds remain in each hand, the position is exactly:
North:  4♦  3♦  2♦
East:   8♦  7♦  6♦
South:  A♦  Q♦  10♦
West:   K♦  J♦  5♦

Why the End Play Beats the Simple Finesse

  • If you finesse early and it loses, the defender returns a suit that may give away a trick.
  • With everything else cleared, the end play forces a defender to lead diamonds (or to give up the lead at a moment that benefits declarer). If West is the player who holds KJ5 and is eventually thrown in, any lead from West hands you the trick(s) — either by leading into South’s AQ10 combination or by giving a ruff-and-sluff equivalent in other contexts.
  • The end play converts “if-my-finesse-works” situations into “if-my-finesse-fails-I-still-get-an-extra-trick” situations.

A Concrete Example at the Three-Card Ending

  1. If South leads the Ace of diamonds, and the defender returns a small diamond, declarer can cash the Queen and then the Ten to make two more tricks — but that sequence depends on who holds the KJ.
  2. Instead, consider tricking so that East or West is forced to lead. If West is thrown in and must lead a diamond, any lead gives the defenders no safe exit — leading into the AQ10 layout gives declarer a trick, and if West leads the K, declarer still takes the Q and T later, emerging with needed tricks.
  3. Thus, even if a direct finesse loses (say you finesse the Q from North at trick 1 and West captures with the K), the opponents are left with a lead that lets declarer exploit the remaining danger and win the contract — the essence of end play.

Wrap-Up and Next Steps

This short example shows the idea in a compact fashion: remove distractions, count winners, and deliberately “throw” a defender into a position where any lead they make gives you something useful. It takes practice to see when to strip suits and when to finesse, but once you can visualize the 3-card ending you’ll start to smell the end plays in the wild.
